It is important to know how Tomb 34 relates to the other burials discovered in Gabii to fully understand how it is both unique and typical of Imperial Roman graves. Another cappuccina grave, a sarcophagus, and an infant burial were also explored.

Tomb 9 - Another Cappuccina Grave

Tomb 9 was found in Area A, and, like Tomb 34, this cappuccina grave was composed of tegulae covering human remains. The vaulted pan tiles were situated with upturned ridges on the exterior and aligned horizontally. This grave was also special in that two fabrics were observed on the tiles, one a pale and coarse fabric and one a fine, red fabric. The female skeleton within ? (find SU of skeleton!)

Tomb 35 - Clay Sarcophagus

Tomb 35 was a clay sarcophagus from Area B. Sarcophagi were an alternative way to bury adults in the Imperial period of Rome. This particular sarcophagus was shaped as a rectangle with a rounded end, in which there was a pillow-like head on which the skeleton’s cranium at one point rested. The lead lid had collapsed, allowing soil to accumulate in the interior of the tomb.

Tomb 32 - Infant Burial

Tomb 32 was an infant burial found in Area B. While cappuccina graves are commonplace for adults, infants were normally buried in vessels, using a burial method known as “enchytrismos.” Infants were buried differently and without grave goods because of their liminal status in society because of the high mortality rate for children. The vessels used in this tomb are two largely intact pots that cap each other. They had been broken, possibly by stones and chunks of other pottery that was found in the fill of the broken vessels. Its also possible that the tomb is associated to the wall alongside which it is buried.
